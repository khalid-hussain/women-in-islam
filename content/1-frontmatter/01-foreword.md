
# Foreword {.unnumbered}

Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Nagramī al-Nadwī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} was among those
distinguished sons of Nadwat al-ʿUlamāʾ who left a profound impression through
their intellectual and practical contributions. In his relatively short life, he
rendered significant service as a scholar of religion and a guide to the
community. Although he departed from this world in the prime of his youth, the
memory of his works and virtues has endured; even today, he is remembered with
great respect and admiration. He belonged to the second generation of Nadwī
scholars and was a contemporary of luminaries such as ʿAllāmah Sayyid Sulaymān
al-Nadwī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} and Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Bārī al-Nadwī
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, among others. These great scholars regarded his passing as
a major loss to scholarship and righteous action, expressing their grief in
eloquent and heartfelt terms.

The intellectual and practical capabilities demonstrated by Mawlānā ʿAbd
al-Raḥmān al-Nagramī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} were marked by depth, benefit, and
scholarly maturity. His writings were published in leading academic journals of
the time. Among them was a notable series on the theme of "Women in Islam,"
which appeared across several issues of Maʿārif. In the modern age, this subject
has often been manipulated to cast aspersions upon Islam, portraying it as
unjust to women and alleging that it deprives them of their rights while
favoring men. On this basis, calls are made to "restore" women's rights
according to non-Islamic frameworks.

However, Islam in reality elevated women from conditions of oppression imposed
by other civilizations, granting them dignity and honor equal to men. Even a
cursory yet fair comparison with other civilizations, whether Roman, Jewish, or
Hindu traditions, reveals that women were often regarded as inferior, reduced to
servitude, or even treated as less than human. In some cultures, they were
likened to embodiments of evil. In contrast, Islam raised women from such
degradation, granting them honor, freeing them from absolute subjugation to men,
and recognizing them as equal partners in dignity. It established their right to
consent in marriage, safeguarded their status within it, and required that they
be honored with a *mahr*, thereby affirming their worth.

Whereas in other societies women could be acquired through force and remained
bound indefinitely, even after a husband's death, often subject to his heirs,
Islam granted them autonomy. It permitted remarriage after widowhood and
allowed, in cases of irreconcilable discord, the dissolution of marriage through
dignified means. Similarly, while daughters in many cultures were considered
burdens, Islam promised immense reward for their upbringing and emphasized their
honorable treatment. It ensured that marriage was not a transaction of
exploitation but a dignified union based on consent, rights, and mutual respect.

Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Nagramī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} presented these
realities in a clear and accessible style, drawing upon the Quran and Ḥadīth to
demonstrate the honor Islam accords to women. He further illustrated how the
women of the early Islamic period embodied these teachings in practice, often
displaying capability equal to, or at times exceeding, that of men, and
contributing meaningfully across various spheres of life. This, he showed, was
not merely theoretical but implemented and exemplified by the Messenger of Allah
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and lived by the women of that era.

He also highlighted that many of the injustices observed in contemporary Muslim
societies stem not from Islamic teachings but from the imitation of non-Islamic
practices. It is therefore the responsibility of Muslims to rectify these
deviations and realign society with the guidance of Islam.

The work produced by Mawlānā ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Nagramī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} on
this subject is of such depth and value that it constitutes a complete book in
itself. It is a matter of satisfaction that a member of his own family, Mawlawī
Muḥammad Faizan al-Nagrāmī al-Nadwī, undertook the effort to publish it (the
Urdu treatise) in book form and requested me to write its foreword, which I have
done. May Allah accept it and make it beneficial. Āmīn.
