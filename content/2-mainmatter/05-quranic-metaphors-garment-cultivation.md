
## Quranic Metaphors: Garment and Cultivation

The importance conveyed by the word “guardian” (_rāʿiyah_) in the _ḥadīth_ is
evident. Ḥujjat al-Hind, Shāh Walī Allāh Muḥaddith al-Dahlawī
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, a great master in the science of the inner wisdoms of the
Sharīʿah, has also discussed this equality of rights in his discourse on
marriage. He states that a human's natural and essential needs are of two types:
some which a person can fulfill independently, and others which require the
assistance of a woman (and similarly, the same applies to a woman). It is for
this reason that the Sharīʿah has declared marriage to be necessary. \[Ḥujjat
Allah al-Bālighah\]

In other words, this implies that a woman is a being who completes humanity. For
this reason, the Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “When a person marries,
he has completed half of his religion.” \[Mishkāt al-Maṣābīḥ\]

This is because human actions are of two types: those related to the Hereafter,
and those related to worldly social life. With the establishment of marriage,
one aspect of social life is, in a sense, completed. Certain *āyāt* of the Noble
Quran further clarify this reality. For example, Allah the Most High says:

[هُنَّ لِبَاسٌ لَّكُمْ وَأَنتُمْ لِبَاسٌ لَّهُنَّ]{dir="rtl"}

They (women) are a garment for you, and you are a garment for them. (Sūrat al-Baqarah, Q2:187)

Clothing is an essential need for human beings, and generally, people take
greater care in its adornment and refinement than in other things; for this
reason, the Noble Quran employs the word *garment* in clarifying the status of
men and women, stating that each of them is a garment for the other. Thus, it
becomes the duty of both man and woman to strive earnestly in beautifying and
refining one another. In another instance, the status of women is presented in
an even more striking manner:

[نِسَاؤُكُمْ حَرْثٌ لَّكُمْ فَأْتُوا حَرْثَكُمْ أَنَّى شِئْتُم]{dir="rtl"}ْ

Your women are a tilth for you, so approach your tilth as you will. (Sūrat al-Baqarah, Q2:223)

According to the scholars of Tafsīr (exegesis), this *āyah* refers to a
particular incident; however, it is a well-established principle of Tafsīr that
the general implications of wording are not invalidated by the specificity of
the occasion. In this *āyah*, women are likened to cultivated land, something
exceedingly precious and beneficial. No farmer ever wishes to ruin his crop;
similarly, men are advised that they should treat their women in the same manner
as a farmer treats his cultivation. This is the distinctive style of expression
of the Quran. Can there be a more profound teaching on harmonious living than
this? The meanings conveyed in both expressions are nearly identical, though
their wording differs -- and in this difference lies a subtle wisdom; however,
before explaining it, it is necessary to establish another foundational point.

In the Noble Quran, the primary audience of its injunctions is the Arabs, and
priority is given to their reform so that one community may first be set upon
the right path, and through it, other nations may be reformed. The Arabs were
chosen for this purpose because their land, from a geographical perspective,
occupies a central position on the globe. For this reason, just as with other
injunctions, the call to reform regarding the rights of women was first directed
toward them. In Arabia, there were two classes of people: one consisted of those
who had no fixed abode---they would move from place to place, cultivate fields,
harvest crops, and then move on elsewhere; the other consisted of those settled
in cities, whose primary occupation was trade.

It is evident that for the former group, nothing is more valuable than
agriculture, while for the latter, outward adornment and refinement take
precedence, the most important element of which is clothing. These two groups
were the direct addressees of the Quran; in order to convey to both groups the
elevated status of women and to instill in their hearts a sense of her worth,
two distinct analogies were employed, and the separate terms *libās* (garment)
and *ḥarth* (tilth) were used. The former addresses the urban population, while
the latter speaks to the Bedouins. Having established this point, we now proceed
to elaborate upon certain matters to which we have previously alluded.
