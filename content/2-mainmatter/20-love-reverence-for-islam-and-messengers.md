
# Love and Reverence for Islam and Allah's Messenger

As you have read in the preceding pages, women sacrificed their lives and wealth
for Islam. Here too, you will see the same reality, though expressed in a
different form. Whenever religion was placed in contrast with any worldly
attachment, they cast aside the latter without hesitation and held firmly to the
command of religion.

When their closest relatives attempted to oppose the Prophet of Islam
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, and sought to use familial ties and kinship as a means of
intercession, these women rejected such appeals with remarkable boldness. They
exerted every effort to uphold the honor and dignity of the Messenger
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}.

After Abū Jahl, Abū Sufyān ibn Harb became the leader of Quraysh. His daughter,
Ramlah (Umm Ḥabībah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}), was among the wives of the
Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. Following the Treaty of Ḥudaybiyyah between the Quraysh
and the Muslims, the people of Makkah soon violated its terms (in just a few
months). Meanwhile, the light of Islam continued to grow stronger, and the
strength and prestige of the Muslims increased steadily. Abū Sufyān feared that
the Muslims might seize an opportunity to attack, so he himself travelled to
Madīnah to renew the treaty. He met Abū Bakr \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, ʿUmar
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, and others, but none agreed to support him.

He desired some form of backing before presenting himself in the court of the
Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, hoping that the intercession of a Muslim might prove
beneficial. He thought, “My daughter is in the household of the Messenger
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, perhaps paternal affection will incline her toward assisting
me.” Thus, he went to her home.

Consider the situation: a father, separated for years, comes to meet his
daughter, fearing for his life, and hopes she will help him. What would be
expected of a daughter? Natural affection would suggest she would embrace him,
weep, and show utmost hospitality. But remember: Abū Sufyān was the leader of
those who sought the life of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and thirsted for his
blood. He was among those whose persecution forced the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} to leave his homeland.

When Abū Sufyān entered, a bedding belonging to the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
was laid out. Instead of honoring her father, the very first thing Umm Ḥabībah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} did was to fold up the bedding. When he asked the reason,
she replied, “You are indeed my father, but you are tainted with disbelief, and
this bedding belongs to the Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. One who is
polluted with the impurity of Shirk has no right to sit upon the bed of the true
Prophet.” \[Sīrat Ibn Hishām\]

A similar incident occurred with ʿUmar \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} when he went to his
sister upon hearing of her acceptance of Islam and demanded to see the Quranic
parchment that Khabbab ibn al-Aratt had been teaching her. She said, “If you
wish to touch it, go and purify yourself first, for this is the Word of Allah,
and its honor must be observed.” \[Sīrat Ibn Hishām\]

Anas ibn Mālik \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reported that after the passing of the
Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, Abū Bakr said to ʿUmar
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, “Let us go to Umm Ayman \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} and visit her
as the Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} used to visit her.” When they
reached her, she began to weep. They said, “What makes you weep? Do you not know
that what is with Allah is better for the Messenger of Allah
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}?”

She replied, “I do not weep because I am unaware that what is with Allah is
better for the Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. Rather, I weep because the
revelation from the heavens has ceased.” This moved them both to tears, and they
began to weep along with her. \[Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim\]

The grief caused by the death of loved ones is immense, yet observe how Muslim
women upheld religion even in such moments. When news of the martyrs of Badr
reached Madīnah, many households were plunged into mourning. Yet, the mother of
Ḥārith ibn Surāqah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} said, “Until the Messenger
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} returns, I cannot grieve for my son. I will ask him whether my
son has attained Paradise through his deeds or has become deserving of
punishment. If he has attained the pleasure of Allah, then there is no reason to
weep; but if he has fallen into punishment, then indeed one should weep for
having ruined his eternal life.” \[Usd al-Ghābah\]

What greater example can there be of love for religion and reverence for faith?
