
# Women in Warfare and Military Service

Women achieved remarkable excellence in the art of warfare. Their contributions
on the battlefield, their zeal in confronting the enemies of Islam, and their
acts of bravery have adorned the pages of history. Numerous examples can be
found wherein, in reality, the crown of victory may rightly be attributed to the
courage and bravery of women. Their spears were ever raised high, and their
swords demonstrated their sharpness in every encounter.

Women not only offered their lives in the path of Allah but also actively
assisted in all the logistical aspects of war: tending to the wounded, caring
for the sick, preparing food for soldiers, and providing water. These duties
were carried out by eminent Ṣaḥābiyyāt, and at times even by the Mothers of the
Believers themselves.

During the Battle of Uḥud, the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} initially sent the
women to a fortified structure under the protection of Ḥassān ibn Thābit
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. When a Jewish man approached for reconnaissance, Ṣafiyyah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} asked Ḥassān to deal with him, but he hesitated. She then
advanced herself with a stick and struck him until he was killed. \[Ṭabaqāt Ibn
Saʿd\]

During the Battle of Uḥud, signs of defeat initially appeared among the Muslims,
and some even fled. In this state of intense emotion, women set out from Madīnah
toward the battlefield. Among them was Ṣafiyyah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, who entered
the ranks with a spear, admonishing the fighters, and encouraging them toward
Jihād. \[Sīrat Ibn Hishām\]

In that same battle, a man named Ibn Qamīʾah shouted, "Where is Muḥammad
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}? If I do not kill him today, may I not live!" Several
Companions, including Muṣʿab ibn ʿUmayr \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, shielded the
Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. Among them was a woman known as Umm ʿUmārah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, a figure of extraordinary courage. She carried a water-skin
on her back, moving through the battlefield to provide water to the thirsty. She
sustained twelve wounds in that battle. Later, during the caliphate of Abū Bakr
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, she fought against Musaylimah and displayed such bravery
that one of her hands was severed, and she received eleven additional wounds.
\[Ṭabaqāt Ibn Saʿd\]

Rufaydah al-Aslamiyyah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} treated the wounded, and a medical
tent was established for her in the mosque. Umm Sulaym \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} also
tended to the wounded at Uḥud and provided water to soldiers. \[Ṭabaqāt Ibn
Saʿd\]

During the caliphate of ʿUmar \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, a fierce battle took
place at Yarmūk against the Romans, whose forces were four times larger. When
Muslim ranks began to retreat and reached the women's tents, the women seized
tent poles and attacked the enemy while simultaneously halting the Muslims,
crying out, "If you do not stand firm, do not return to us!" Their courage
restored the ranks of the Muslims, who stood firm and ultimately defeated the
Romans.

During the same battle, a woman named Asmāʾ al-Anṣāriyyah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}
killed nine disbelievers using nothing more than a simple wooden stick. \[Tārīkh
al-Ṭabarī\]

Asmāʾ bint Abī Bakr \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} displayed such valor that she
rode alongside her husband Zubayr \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, entering enemy
ranks and turning the tide of battle. When he struck, she too struck, and with
each blow, a head would fall.[^8]

[^8]: Translator's Note: This narration appears to be a misattribution or
    exaggeration and cannot be cited as an authentic historical report. The
    report is likely influenced by confusion with Nusaybah bint Kaʿb
    \hon{\P_HER}{\FOOTER}.

During the Umayyad period, when Ḥajjāj besieged ʿAbdullāh ibn al-Zubayr
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, he sought counsel from his mother, Asmāʾ
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}. She delivered a powerful and impassioned address, saying,

> O my beloved son! You understand your own interests well. But if you are
> firmly convinced that the path you have chosen is not in opposition to the
> Commands of Allah nor beyond His prescribed limits, then you must remain
> steadfast. If you are killed, you will attain the rank of martyrdom. But if
> your aim is worldly power and ambition, then none is worse than you -- you
> destroy yourself and ruin others. Indeed, your argument that you are alone may
> be correct, and in a state of solitude, there may be no option except
> obedience, but this is not the conduct of noble people. Tell me, how long will
> you live in this world? When it is certain that the moment of death is fixed
> and can neither be advanced nor delayed, then it is far better that a person
> depart this world with honor. \[Ṭabaqāt Ibn Saʿd\]

Every word of her speech was filled with force and impact, and each sentence was
like an armed battalion piercing the heart.

From these examples, it becomes clear that women did not merely participate in
warfare out of religious zeal in moments of necessity. Rather, they underwent
proper training in the arts of war. They took part in battles as accomplished
swordswomen and skilled archers, and their aim was precise and accurate.

For example, when the husband of Umm Abān \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} was killed
by the Roman general Tūmā during the Battle of Damascus, she vowed to avenge him
personally. Many Muslim soldiers assured her that they would avenge their fallen
brother against the Romans, but this courageous woman replied, "I shall take
revenge myself, and I will bring about the end of Tūmā with my own weapon."

When the battle resumed, General Tūmā appeared in the center of the army,
surrounded on all sides by dense ranks of troops like swarms of locusts, making
access to him extremely difficult. Therefore, Umm Abān took aim with an arrow
and released it with such precision that it struck directly into his eye,
bringing about his death.

Umm Ḥakīm \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} killed seven disbelievers in the Battle of
Ajnādayn using nothing more than a tent pole. \[Usd al-Ghābah\]

Khawlah bint al-Azwar, the sister of the renowned commander, Ḍirār, led a
special contingent of women in the Battle of Antioch and displayed remarkable
bravery in the Battle of Yarmūk. \[Futūḥ al-Shām\]

Rabīʿ bint Muʿawwidh \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} would personally participate in
Jihād, supply water to soldiers, and carry the martyrs from the battlefield back
to Madīnah al-Munawwarah.[-@@bukhari, vol. 5, p. 129, #5679]

During the pledge of al-ʿAqabah, women played a significant role. They would
actively encourage one another to participate. For example, during the Battle of
Badr, many women joined the battlefield due to the encouragement of Amīnah
al-Ghifāriyyah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}.[^9]

[^9]: Translator's Note: There is no reliable report that establishes groups of
    women accompanied the army at Badr. Women are clearly documented in the
    Battles of Uḥud, Khandaq, and later expeditions.

Khansāʾ bint ʿAmr, the famous Arab poetess, would recite her poetry before the
Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, who would listen with great appreciation. She
participated in the Battle of Qādisiyyah, and her four sons were present. She
delivered a powerful and stirring address to them, and all four attained
martyrdom. \[Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī\]

In summary, if the military services of women were to be detailed fully, volumes
upon volumes would be required. These few examples suffice to demonstrate that
they never lagged behind in any service to their community and nation.
