
## **Deficiency of Intellect (Nuqṣān al-ʿAql) and the Question of Female Authority**

There is no doubt that the Prophet Muḥammad [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} counted women among
those described as deficient in intellect (*nāqiṣāt ʿaql*). He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
was truthful and divinely guided; to turn away from his testimony would be the
height of misfortune and deprivation. Undoubtedly, he was the true Messenger of
Allah. He came to entrust the world, for the final time, with the keys to divine
treasures. He was sent solely to proclaim the message of Divine Unity and to
convey the final message of the Lord of all worlds.

The Sharīʿah he brought is the ultimate law of civilization and refinement; it
is a scale of justice and equity in which the deeds of all are weighed without
bias.

Given this, how can it be claimed that he [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} intended to degrade and
dishonor women to such an extent? Or that his statement regarding their
"deficiency of intellect" should be interpreted in the way some people loudly
assert today---namely, that women should not be given higher education because
they are "deficient in intellect," that they might change their religion, or
that they should not be allowed access to knowledge lest they neglect household
responsibilities?

God forbid! This is not the intent of the Prophet's [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} statement.
Those who possess insight, who have the full *ḥadīth* before them regarding
"deficiency of intellect," and who are familiar with the judgments and legal
decisions of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, can never bear witness to such an
interpretation.

Consider this: Jewish women would come to the Mother of the Believers ʿĀʾishah
[رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}, sit with her, converse with her, and the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} did not prohibit this. Today, if women were to sit with women of
other communities for the purpose of learning various sciences and disciplines,
it would be counted among the prohibitions. (If this is indeed the case), then
yes, this is your own failing: that you do not provide your children with such
firm religious education that they would not lose their faith at the slightest
influence.

In any case, our present discussion concerns the claim that women are being
described as "deficient in intellect". First, we present the complete *ḥadīth*
from *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī*, after which we will explain it. The original ḥadīth is
as follows:

Abū Saʿīd al-Khuḍrī [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} narrated that the Messenger of
Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} went out on the occasion of either ʿĪd al-Aḍḥá or ʿĪd
al-Fiṭr to the place of prayer (*muṣallá*). He then passed by the women and
said, "O assembly of women, give charity, for I have been shown that you form
the majority of the people of the Fire." They said, "And why is that, O
Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}?"

He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} replied, "You frequently curse, and you are ungrateful to your
husbands. I have not seen anyone more deficient in intelligence and religion,
yet more capable of overcoming the understanding of a resolute man, than one of
you." They said, "O Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, what is the deficiency in
our intelligence and our religion?"

He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} said, "Is not the testimony of a woman equal to half the
testimony of a man?" They replied, "Yes." He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} said, "That is the
deficiency of her intellect. Is it not the case that when she menstruates, she
neither prays nor fasts?" They replied, "Yes." He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} said, "That is
the deficiency in her religion." \[Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī\]

The wording of this *ḥadīth* is clear and explicit. The Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
did not use any general expression regarding the deficiency of intellect that
could be applied to all matters universally. If women were deficient in
intellect in every domain, there would have been no need for the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} to specify particular cases. Instead, he explicitly linked this
"deficiency" to a specific legal context---namely, the matter of testimony
(shahādah).

In the specific matter of testimony, the designation of deficiency is entirely
consistent with natural disposition (*fiṭrah*). Women are generally occupied
with the finer details of domestic life; they are tender-hearted and, as such,
do not typically engage in deep, sustained deliberation over disputes and
conflicts. Instead, much of their time is spent responding emotionally to
events. Therefore, if their testimony alone were relied upon in such cases, the
resolution of disputes might not always be precise.

Shāh Walī Allah [رحمه الله]{dir="rtl"} writes in his commentary on the Muwaṭṭaʾ
that in the Sharīʿah, the testimony of two women is equated with that of one man
in certain legal matters because women's memory is generally more susceptible to
lapse. By pairing two women together, one supports the other in recalling events
accurately, thereby ensuring the completeness of testimony. \[Al-Musawwā\]

At the same time, the blessed practice of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
demonstrates that this principle is not absolute in all matters. In issues
specifically related to women, he did not require this numerical condition;
rather, he would at times decide based on the testimony of a single woman.

For example, the incident of ʿUqbah ibn al-Ḥārith as reported in *Ṣaḥīḥ
al-Bukhārī*. When he intended to marry a woman, another woman came forward and
claimed that she had breastfed both of them. Since the required number of
witnesses was not present, he did not consider it sufficient and traveled to
Madīnah to verify the matter. The Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} said, "How can it be
permissible when such a claim has already been made?"

It is clear that if women were deficient in all matters, the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} would not have issued such a ruling on this basis. In this
*ḥadīth*, the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} also indicated a unique quality of women.
The very opening of the *ḥadīth* shows this: despite being described as
deficient in intellect, women possess the ability to captivate and overpower
even resolute men.

This points to a dimension of human perfection in which man depends upon woman
to complete his own humanity. It is an affirmation of the idea that a woman is a
being through whom the completion of human life is realized.

The explanation given by the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} regarding deficiency was
limited to specific contexts. However, later people expanded these meanings far
beyond their intended scope. In reality, such an expansion is entirely contrary
to the established rhetorical norms of the Arabic language.

Consider this analogy: when Allah portrays the fluctuating, unstable, and
changing condition of (human beings, He says that man is "despairing and
ungrateful" ([يَؤُوسٌ كَفُور]{dir="rtl"}ٌ). Now, if someone were to argue from
this that human beings should have no connection with hope or gratitude at all,
because Allah has described them as such, would that be acceptable? Certainly
not. This is because the context of the discourse necessitates such wording when
depicting a particular state.

Likewise, when the Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} described women as
deficient in intellect and religion in certain matters, those words were
appropriate to that context. It cannot, by any means, imply that women are
deficient in intellect in every matter. If that were the case, the Messenger
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} would not have described them as the best provision of the world.

Some people advance another argument: that since women are deficient in
intellect, the Lawgiver has deprived them of governance and political authority.
In support of this claim, they cite the *ḥadīth*, "A people who entrust their
affairs to a woman will never prosper. \[Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī\]

However, this is only a fragment of the ḥadīth. When the complete narration is
examined, its reality becomes clear. In *Sunan al-Nasāʾī*, it is reported:

It was narrated from Abū Bakrah [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}, "Allah benefitted me
with something that I heard from the Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}. When
Kisrā (the Persian Emperor) perished, he [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} said, 'Whom did they
appoint as his successor?' They said, 'His daughter.' He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} said, 'A
people who entrust their affairs to a woman will not prosper.'"

In reality, this was a prophecy regarding the great conquest that the Muslims
would achieve over the Persian Empire. It was not intended as a universal,
absolute legal ruling to exclude women from all forms of governance
categorically. Had the objective been a blanket prohibition of women in
leadership, then the discussion would not have been tied specifically to that
historical incident involving the succession of Kisrā.
