
## **Protection of Women\'s Rights**

In order to demonstrate that Islam and its Caller [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} raised women
from that deep pit of degradation into which all nations of the world had
collectively cast them, we now present a few historical examples. These
illustrate how, once Islam enabled this delicate creation to stand on its own
feet and declared:

[وَلَهُنَّ مِثْلُ الَّذِي عَلَيْهِنَّ بِالْمَعْرُوفِ]{dir="rtl"}

*And for them are rights similar to those upon them, according to what is just,*
\[Sūrat Al-Baqarah, Q2:228\]

Women then, with remarkable freedom and courage, protected their rights,
presented their claims before the supreme court of Islam, and obtained their
judgments. Freedom, truthfulness, love of justice, and frankness are general
teachings of Islam, and the foreheads of its followers are illuminated by these
radiant qualities. The mercy of Islam is not confined to any particular group or
gender; women, too, benefited from it, and benefited greatly. By way of example,
a few incidents are recorded.

*Mahr* is a legal right of women. In the *Ḥadīth*, a minimum of ten dirhams is
mentioned, while no upper limit has been fixed; rather, women themselves possess
the right to stipulate it. The Commander of the Faithful, ʿUmar [رضي الله
عنه]{dir="rtl"}, once investigated the amounts of *mahr*, and upon learning that
the *mahr* of Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} was of a certain
amount, he ascended the pulpit and declared, "Do not be excessive in the dowries
of women."

He further stated that whoever exceeds this limit, the excess amount would be
taken into the public treasury. At that very gathering, a woman raised her voice
in opposition and said, "When Allah, the Most High, has said, 'If you have given
one of them a great amount (a heap) of wealth, then do not take anything back
from it,' (Sūrat An-Nisā' Q4:20) then what right does the Commander of the
Faithful have to confiscate this excess?

ʿUmar [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} immediately accepted her argument and
spontaneously said, "A woman has spoken correctly, and a man has erred."
\[Muṣannaf 'Abd al-Razzāq\]

From this incident, numerous conclusions may be drawn, including the powerful
effect of Islamic teachings and the truthfulness and justice of the successors
of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}. However, these are beyond our present subject.
What is relevant here is that an ordinary woman, with remarkable boldness,
defended her rightful claim in opposition to the command of the Caliph and
succeeded.

This incident occurred after the era of Prophethood, but an even more remarkable
event took place in the very presence of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} himself.
Barīrah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} was a bondwoman whom ʿĀʾishah al-Ṣiddīqah
[رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} purchased and set free. Before her emancipation, she
had been married to a slave named Mughīth. Barīrah was not pleased with him.
According to Islamic law, a freed bondwoman has the right, after emancipation,
to either remain with her previous husband or to separate from him. Barīrah
exercised her right and annulled the marriage. Mughīth, however, loved her
deeply; after the separation, he would wander through the streets in distress,
weeping loudly, yet Barīrah paid no heed to his lamentations and refused to
remarry him.

When the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} saw Mughīth's condition, he said to Barīrah, "It
would be better if you returned to him." Barīrah responded, "O Messenger of
Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, are you commanding me?"

He [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} replied, "I am only interceding, not commanding." Barīrah then
said, "I have no need of him." \[Sunan al-Nasāʾī\]
