
## A Brief Discussion of Certain Rights

It is not possible here to detail all the rights that Islam has granted women,
for that would require a separate treatise, and indeed a substantial body of
literature already exists in Urdu on this subject; however, we shall briefly
discuss a few.

### The Right of Consent in Marriage

Among the many injustices inflicted upon women, one of the greatest is that in
matters of marriage, which constitute the essence of their lives, they are given
no authority, whereas the *aḥādīth* and rulings of the Sharīʿah clearly indicate
otherwise. As mentioned earlier, the contract of marriage is the right of women,
and they should be granted full freedom in this matter.

The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said,

[الأَيِّمُ أَحَقُّ بِنَفْسِهَا مِنْ وَلِيِّهَا، وَالْبِكْرُ تُسْتَأْذَنُ فِي
نَفْسِهَا، وَإِذْنُهَا صُمَاتُهَا]{dir="rtl"}

A previously married woman (_ayyim_) has more right over herself. \[Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim\]

The term _ayyim_ in this _ḥadīth_ refers to a woman without a husband, whether a
virgin or previously married; thus, both require their consent to be sought in
marriage. This ruling itself makes it evident that the matter of marriage
pertains to women, and that guardians act only after seeking their permission.
How then could it be possible that in our society this command is disregarded,
to the extent that marriages are conducted without their consent?

The greatest error in this regard is that parents pay no heed to the approval
and satisfaction of their daughters, nor do they consult them in such matters.

### The Concept of Mahr

Islam has granted women a specific right known as *mahr*. In many lands, and
even among certain communities in India, the opposite custom prevails, whereby
wealth is given by the woman's side to the man; however, this contradicts the
natural principle of division of roles and distribution of virtues. Some have
even suggested that *mahr* is compensation for the benefit the man derives, but
in reality, men are given a degree of authority as heads of the household, and
in return, the Sharīʿah grants women financial entitlement so that balance is
maintained and neither party holds unqualified superiority over the other.

Allah says,

[خَلَقَكُم مِّن نَّفْسٍ وَاحِدَةٍ]{dir="rtl"}

He created you all from a single soul. (Sūrat al-Nisāʾ, Q4:1)

Since both genders originate from one essence, granting one party authority over
another without any form of compensation runs contrary to the principle of
equity. For this reason, the Sharīʿah has established a balanced framework: just
as, through marriage, a woman comes under a certain degree of authority of the
man, an arrangement that constitutes a form of advantage, so, in return, women
are granted a corresponding distinction in the form of a specified financial
entitlement (*mahr*). In this way, each party is given a distinct right and form
of merit, ensuring that neither holds an absolute advantage over the other. The
balance of rights prevents any unjust superiority from remaining with one side
alone. \[Tafsīr al-Manār\]

For this reason, after mentioning such rulings, the Quran declares:

[وَلَا تَتَمَنَّوْا مَا فَضَّلَ اللَّهُ بِهِ بَعْضَكُمْ عَلَىٰ
بَعْضٍ]{dir="rtl"}[]{dir="ltr"}

Do not desire that by which Allah has given some of you preference over others.
(Sūrat al-Nisāʾ, Q4:32)

And further:

[لِلرِّجَالِ نَصِيبٌ مِّمَّا اكْتَسَبُوا وَلِلنِّسَاءِ نَصِيبٌ مِّمَّا
اكْتَسَبْنَ]{dir="rtl"}

For men is a share of what they earn, and for women is a share of what they
earn. (Sūrat al-Nisāʾ, Q4:32)

Thus, it is contrary to principle that the woman should give compensation to the
man, for this would amount to granting men superiority without balance.
Similarly, in some regions, dowry is wrongly considered a right of men, leading
to disputes and conflicts, whereas in reality it is merely a gift given by
parents to their daughter, dependent entirely upon their means.

### The Right of Inheritance

Among the greatest favors Islam has bestowed upon women is the right of
inheritance. Many nations have deprived women of the right to inheritance.
However, Islam has included nearly all female relatives among the *dhawī
al-furūḍ*---that is, those heirs whose shares are fixed and made obligatory by
the Sharīʿah.

Among the prescribed shares in Islamic law, the largest portion is two-thirds
(2/3) of the estate, and this share has been assigned exclusively to women in
specific cases. The wisdom behind this is clear: men, by divine decree, are
generally endowed with the means and opportunities for earning and providing
livelihood, whereas women are often occupied with the demanding responsibilities
of managing the household.

As a result, women are not always afforded the same opportunities to earn a
living (though it is permissible for them). Therefore, the Sharīʿah has granted
them a substantial share of inheritance so that they may remain financially
secure and free from anxiety regarding provision. This enables them to dedicate
themselves to the nurturing and upbringing of their children, thereby raising
them into upright and valuable members of society.

### The Question of Education

Among the discussions relating to women, the most significant and complex issue
is that of women's education. The ongoing debate between the modernists and the
traditionalists owes much of its intensity to this very question. Many elders
have attempted to cast it in a purely religious light and have permitted, at
most, only a minimal level of education for women. It is regrettable, however,
that such individuals neither examined the guidance of Islam with depth, nor
reflected upon the practice of Muḥammad, the Messenger of Allah
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, nor gave due consideration to the perspectives of the
Ṣaḥābah \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY} and the Tābiʿīn \hon{\M_THM}{\BODY}. Had they done
so, it would have become evident to them how widespread and vigorous the pursuit
of knowledge among women was in the earliest generations.

The Mothers of the Believers \hon{\P_THMF}{\BODY} and the female Companions
achieved remarkable excellence; their rank in knowledge and learning was
distinguished and elevated. The table of Islam's generosity was not spread
exclusively for men; women were equal participants in it. Who can deny the
knowledge and intellectual excellence of ʿĀʾishah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}? The most
eminent among the Ṣaḥābah and Tābiʿīn would seek guidance from her in matters of
religion. Numerous narrations record her precise and insightful corrections even
upon great Companions such as ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY},
demonstrating the depth of her understanding. She was counted among the
*mujtahidīn* of the Ṣaḥābah. Nor was she alone in this regard; the other Mothers
of the Believers \hon{\P_THMF}{\BODY} were also well-versed in the Quran and the
Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. Thus, in reality, the
education of women is itself a Sunnah, the fulfillment of which is incumbent
upon us.

It is narrated regarding Umm Salamah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} that once the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said that none among those who participated in the pledge
beneath the tree (at Al-Ḥudaybiyyah) would enter the Fire. She immediately
responded by citing the Quran:

[وَإِنْ مِنْكُمْ إِلَّا وَارِدُهَا]{dir="rtl"}

There is none among you except that he will pass over it. (Sūrat Maryam,
Q19:71)

Upon hearing this, the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} clarified his statement
through another Quran *āyah*. Consider whether, in our time, women are educated
to such a degree that they may raise such questions and seek their resolution
from the scholars of the community. If they are not, then it must be said that
the Sunnah of Muḥammad \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} is being neglected. Indeed, if one
speaks the truth, a major cause of the decline of Muslims is the ignorance of
women.

In earlier times, generations were nurtured in the laps of women who, in
reality, nourished them with knowledge and culture, instilling within them the
spirit of religion and community. From the era of Prophethood and for centuries
thereafter, it remained a common practice that women would conduct formal
lessons; people would benefit from them and would take pride in sitting before
them as students.

Imam Abū Dāwūd al-Sijistānī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, whose *Sunan* is counted among
the six canonical collections of Ḥadīth, himself learned from a female teacher.
In the list of teachers of al-ʿAllāmah al-Suyūṭī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, many
accomplished female scholars are found. Even a figure as eminent as ʿUmar ibn
al-Khaṭṭāb \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} would consult Asmāʾ bint ʿUmays
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} regarding the interpretation of dreams. Some women attained
such mastery in delivering lectures that they were given distinguished titles;
Asmāʾ bint Sakan \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} was commonly known as *Khaṭībat al-Anṣār*
(the Orator of the Anṣār).

Similarly, from Nusaybah bint Kaʿb \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, both the Ṣaḥābah and the
Tābiʿīn learned the proper method of the rites of washing the deceased.

All of these examples make it evident that the disagreements regarding women's
education are largely rooted in personal opinions. The actual practice of the
Ṣaḥābah and Tābiʿīn stands in clear contrast to such restrictions. Some
advocates of modern education cite the *ḥadīth*, "Seeking knowledge is
obligatory upon every Muslim man and Muslim woman."

It is true that the addition “...and every Muslim woman...” (_wa muslimah_) is
not established as authentic; however, the inclusion of that word is not
necessary to prove the obligation of education for women. The Messenger of Allah
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim.”
\[Sunan Ibn Mājah\]

The word Muslim itself is general and not restricted to men alone. This is
consistent with the Quranic and Prophetic style, where general commands are
often addressed to men while including women in their scope. This is further
confirmed by another narration: some women once came to the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} to ask specific questions but felt shy; upon realizing this,
he \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} instructed them not to feel ashamed in seeking knowledge
and said, “Seeking knowledge is obligatory upon every Muslim.” \[Sunan Ibn
Mājah\]

This narration is also recorded in *Sharḥ al-Muwaṭṭaʾ* by Shāh Walī Allāh
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}. It clearly indicates that the term includes both men and
women.

Imam al-Bukhārī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} dedicated a separate chapter to the
education of women, which shows the importance of this issue even in that era.
The Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} consistently emphasized education on
various occasions. The Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “A man who
has a slave-girl, then he disciplines her, educates her well, then frees her and
marries her, will have a double reward.” \[Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī\]

Though this ruling pertains to bondwomen, it clearly demonstrates the importance
attached to education. Indeed, the very manner in which education is mentioned
shows how greatly the Messenger of Allah \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} valued the education
of women. In conclusion, the education of women is an essential and
indispensable matter. No prohibition has been issued against it; rather,
numerous indications throughout the sources point to its necessity.

An even more significant matter than reading is writing, for which evidence from
the Prophetic era also exists. While those of a conservative disposition
strongly oppose it, a narration in *Sunan Abī Dāwūd* indicates that the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} appointed a person specifically to teach Ḥafṣah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} the art of writing. On this basis, leading scholars have
issued verdicts permitting it. In our view, this is not merely permissible but
also a Sunnah upon which Muslims ought to act.

As for the scholarly accomplishments of women, we intend, *InshāʾAllāh*, to
present them in detail in a separate treatise.
