
## **Specific Virtues (Faḍāʾil Makhsūṣah)**

When general commentators discuss mutual distinction, they enumerate many
virtues of men, such as prophethood, caliphate, judiciary, and the like. The
matters of caliphate and judiciary themselves are such that scholars differ
regarding them. The investigation of prophethood will be written under a
separate heading. For the intellectual interest of Muslim women, we present a
few of their specific virtues, so that it may be known that just as men have
been granted many distinctions, likewise their domain too is filled with certain
virtues and special qualities, some of which are directly connected to the
blessed and noble person of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}.

Based on our knowledge, we may say that no man can lay claim to these virtues.
The Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} was a mercy for all the worlds. Just as he
conveyed to us a speech so sacred and exalted that it serves as a healer for our
spiritual ailments and a remover of the weaknesses of the heart, likewise Allah,
the Lord of Majesty, also informed us, through the mediation of the Messenger
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, of the means to repel physical illnesses and the supplications
associated with them. Just as the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} was a spiritual
physician, he was likewise a healer of bodily ailments.

The books of *ḥadīth* record numerous instances in which people would present
their complaints, and the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} would teach them *āyāt* of the
Quran and supplications as remedies. It is also evident that many individuals,
driven by various needs, would come into his presence, and he [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
would pray for their healing or prescribe for them certain *āyāt* of the Noble
Qur'an. All of this is well established.

However, did it ever occur that someone recited over the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
himself and performed *ruqyah* on him? The works of many are entirely silent on
this point. However, the right to claim this virtue belongs to women, for one of
its members recited *āyāt* of the Quran over the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} in
accordance with his own teachings and performed *ruqyah* on him.

ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} relates that during the time of the
Prophet's [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} final illness, he would recite the Muʿawwidhatayn:

[قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ الْفَلَقِ...]{dir="rtl"}

[قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ النَّاسِ...]{dir="rtl"}

*Say: I seek refuge in the Lord of daybreak.* \[Sūrat al-Falaq, Q113\]

*Say: I seek refuge in the Lord of mankind.* \[Sūrat al-Nās, Q114\]

...and would blow over himself. When his illness was aggravated, she used to
recite those two Sūrahs and blow her breath over him and make him rub his body
with his own hand for its blessings. \[Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī\]

The passing of the Master of the Universe [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} took place in the lap
of a woman. ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} said, "Allah took his soul while
he was between my chest and my neck, and he was buried in my house." \[Ṣaḥīḥ
al-Bukhārī\]

When ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} would mention her virtues in comparison
to the other wives of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, she would include this among
them. Even today, this honor remains a distinction of the gentler gender; can
any man claim such?

The Master of all creations [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} did not, in matters of transactions
(such as buying and selling), appoint anyone with a particular distinction as
his representative. However, this honor belongs to the gentler gender, because
the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} himself first undertook trade to Syria as Khadījah\'s
[رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} representative[^6].

We have already stated at the outset that such personal virtues do not, in
reality, establish superiority or precedence. However, since human dispositions
generally incline toward such distinctions, and such qualities are often
mentioned, we have written these few lines merely for the interest of women.
