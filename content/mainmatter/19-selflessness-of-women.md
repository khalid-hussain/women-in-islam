
## **The Selflessness of Muslim Women**

Just as women stood alongside men in all the noble qualities of the early
generations, they were not behind in the quality of self-sacrifice (*īthār*).
This trait was among the distinguishing features of that era. The Quran
highlights,

[وَيُؤْثِرُونَ عَلَىٰ أَنفُسِهِمْ وَلَوْ كَانَ بِهِمْ خَصَاصَةٌ]{dir="rtl"}

*"They prefer others over themselves, even though they themselves are in need."*
\[Sūrat al-Ḥashr, Q59:9\]

In this path, the gentler gender traversed such difficult stages that even among
men, examples of similar magnitude are rarely found. Women are known for their
deep love for their children, yet for the sake of the call of Islam and their
true religion, they sacrificed even their beloved offspring.

Umm Sulaym [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}, whose incident you have already read, was
extremely poor. When the Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} migrated to Madīnah,
every person presented whatever gift they could, for that day was one of immense
joy and celebration for them. The greatest and most blessed favor had set foot
in their land, and the sun of Prophethood had risen in the valleys of Madinah.
On that blessed day, the people of Madīnah came out in great numbers to welcome
him, and young girls joyfully recited:

[طَلَعَ الْبَدْرُ عَلَيْنَا مِنْ ثَنِيَّاتِ الْوَدَاعِ]{dir="rtl"}

[وَجَبَ الشُّكْرُ عَلَيْنَا مَا دَعَا لِلّٰهِ دَاعِ]{dir="rtl"}

*The full moon has risen upon us from the valley of Thaniyyāt al-Wadāʿ;\
Gratitude has become obligatory upon us so long as one calls to Allah.*

Umm Sulaym [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} also wished to present a gift but was
constrained by poverty. Finally, she brought her young son, Anas [رضي الله
عنه]{dir="rtl"}, and presented him to the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, saying, "I
offer him to you. Keep him in your service, and supplicate for him for long life
and abundance of wealth." The Messenger [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} accepted this gift with
pleasure and supplicated for Anas [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}.

This is the same Anas [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} who served the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} for ten years, lived to the age of ninety-one, and later governed
Baṣrah during the caliphate of ʿUmar [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}. What greater
example of sacrifice for religion than that a poor woman offered the very piece
of her heart and beloved child in dedication to Islam?

Among the campaigns of the Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, the Day of Uḥud
holds a distinct renown. This battle was, in many respects, a great trial for
the Muslims. The renowned leader of the Muslims and one of the most valiant
warriors of Arabia, Ḥamzah [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}, attained martyrdom in this
very battle. He was killed by Wahshī, the slave of Hind bint ʿUtbah, who struck
him with a spear. It was the same Wahshī who later killed Musaylimah the Liar in
the Battle of Yamāmah. His famous statement is well-documented: "At Uḥud I
killed the best of people, and at Yamāmah I killed the worst of people."

Hind bint ʿUtbah mutilated the body of Sayyid al-Shuhadāʾ (Ḥamzah [رضي الله
عنه]{dir="rtl"}); she took out his liver, chewed it, and severed various parts
of his body. The scene was so heart-rending and grief-stricken that any human
being would naturally lose composure. For this reason, the Messenger
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} instructed that his aunt Ṣafiyyah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}
should not be allowed to see the body, lest she be unable to bear it.

However, when Ṣafiyyah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} learned of this and came to
see her brother's body, her son Zubayr [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} (the disciple
of the Messenger [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}) conveyed the Prophet's instruction to her. Yet
she replied, "I am aware that my brother Ḥamzah [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} has
been mutilated. Why do you stop me? To be sacrificed in the path of Allah in
such a manner is no extraordinary matter." \[Ṭabaqāt Ibn Saʿd\]

Women of that age might have filled the heavens with cries of lamentation, but
this lion-hearted woman showed not even a crease upon her brow. Such is the
affair of Islam, that any hardship or suffering in the path of Allah is borne
with serenity and composure:

[آنچه از دوست می‌رسد نیکوست]{dir="rtl"}

*"Whatever comes from the Beloved is good."*

In one battle, all the relatives of an Anṣārī woman were martyred. When people
came to inform her, her first question was, "Is the Messenger [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
safe?" When she was given the glad tidings of his safety, she was overwhelmed
with joy and said, "If the Noble Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} is safe, then thousands
of lives may be sacrificed for him." \[Sīrat Ibn Hishām\]

Similarly, when the Messenger [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} returned safely to Madīnah after
the Battle of Uḥud, despite having sustained multiple wounds, Kabshah bint
Rāfiʿ, the mother of Saʿd ibn Muʿādh [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}, came running and
fell at his feet, saying, "In comparison to your safety and well-being,
everything else is insignificant."

Umm Sulaym [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} forgave her *mahr* merely because her
husband accepted Islam. \[Sunan al-Nasāʾī\]

Among these accounts, the incident of Asmāʾ bint Abī Bakr [رضي الله
عنها]{dir="rtl"} is especially remarkable. When the Messenger [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
intended to migrate from Makkah, provisions were prepared at the house of Abū
Bakr [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}. In haste, nothing was available to tie the food.
Asmāʾ [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} immediately tore her waistband into two pieces,
using one to tie the provisions and the other to tie herself. From that day, she
became known as Dhāt al-Niṭāqayn (the woman of the two waistbands). She was a
woman of great courage and strength. ʿAbdullāh ibn al-Zubayr [رضي الله
عنهما]{dir="rtl"} was born to her. \[Sīrat Ibn Hishām\]

Greater still is the immense sacrifice demonstrated by the Mothers of the
Believers (Ummahāt al-Muʾminīn). When some of the wives of the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} made certain requests, the following *āyah* was revealed:

[يَا أَيُّهَا النَّبِيُّ قُلْ لِّأَزْوَاجِكَ إِن كُنتُنَّ تُرِدْنَ الْحَيَاةَ
الدُّنْيَا وَزِينَتَهَا فَتَعَالَيْنَ أُمَتِّعْكُنَّ وَأُسَرِّحْكُنَّ سَرَاحًا
جَمِيلًا]{dir="rtl"}

[وَإِن كُنتُنَّ تُرِدْنَ اللّٰهَ وَرَسُولَهُ وَالدَّارَ الْآخِرَةَ فَإِنَّ
اللّٰهَ أَعَدَّ لِلْمُحْسِنَاتِ مِنكُنَّ أَجْرًا عَظِيمًا]{dir="rtl"}

*O Prophet! Say to your wives, "If you desire the life of this world and its
adornment, then come, I will give you a (suitable) compensation (for divorce)
and let you go graciously. But if you desire Allah, His Messenger, and the
Hereafter, then indeed Allah has prepared for the righteous among you a great
reward."* \[Sūrat al-Aḥzāb, Q33:28--29\]

This was an extremely severe test. When this *āyah* was revealed, the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} first went to ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} and said, "I
wish to say something to you, but do not hasten in your reply; consult your
parents first." After explaining the matter, ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}
replied, "In such matters, there is no need to consult my parents. I choose
Allah and His Messenger." \[Sunan Ibn Mājah\]

Thereafter, the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} went to the other wives and informed them
of ʿĀʾishah's response, and all of them affirmed the same choice.

After this profound example, can it not be said that women have left behind
unparalleled and unmatched examples of sacrifice in the history of Islam?

Even ordinary women upheld this spirit. Once, a woman named Saʿīrah al-Asadiyyah
[رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} came to the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} complaining of
illness and requested a supplication for a cure. The Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
said, "If you wish, I will supplicate for you, and you will be cured; but if you
exercise patience, Allah will grant you a great reward." She immediately
replied, "I choose the reward of Allah." \[Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī\]

One of the paternal aunts of the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, ʿArwá bint ʿAbd
al-Muṭṭalib [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}, had a son named Ṭayyib. When he accepted
Islam, Abū Jahl and other leaders of Quraysh tied him to a pillar and beat him.
People came to his mother and said, "Had he not supported Muḥammad
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, this would not have happened." Yet she paid no heed to such
complaints and would proudly say, "The best day of his life is the day he
supports his cousin."

Reflect upon all these narrations and consider: is it not correct to say that
women not only stood alongside men in this virtue, but in some cases even
surpassed them? Women of the present age should reflect deeply upon these
incidents. Just as their predecessors demonstrated such sacrifice in service of
religion and religious honor, they too must dedicate their intellectual and
physical capacities with zeal and enthusiasm toward the progress of their
community and the rescue of its declining condition. By spending generously from
their wealth in collective causes, they should demonstrate that the same
religious fervor and communal spirit still flows within them.

[نِعْمَ الْمَوْلَىٰ وَنِعْمَ النَّصِيرُ]{dir="rtl"}[]{dir="ltr"}

*"Excellent is the Protector, and excellent is the Helper."* \[Sūrat al-Anfāl,
Q8:40\]
