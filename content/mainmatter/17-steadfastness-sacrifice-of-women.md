
## Steadfastness and the Sacrifice of Muslim Women

Up to this point, what we have written are indications from religious
discussions commonly associated with women. Now we turn to the historical
dimension, to show that this delicate creation has stood shoulder to shoulder
with men in every plain of hardship and test.

At the dawn of Islam

- When men left their beloved homelands, women accompanied them.
- When men stood armed against the deniers, women too displayed courage and
  resolve.
- When men sacrificed their lives for the sake of faith, women also offered the
  greatest sacrifices.
- When men endured the harshest trials while proclaiming the name of Allah,
  women too, even under the blade, proclaimed the same truth.

The reality is that those blessed souls into whom the first breath of Islam was
infused were among the foremost in enduring divine trials. The hardships they
faced were unmatched by the followers of any Prophet. As a result, Allah granted
them dominion over the earth.

But do not imagine that this honor belonged to men alone. Women, too, displayed
the same steadfastness, the same zeal, and the same resolve. In some respects,
the gentler gender even possesses precedence in certain distinctions, which will
be detailed under the discussion of *Awwaliyyāt al-Nisāʾ* (Firsts of Women).

The remarkable incident of the Islam of ʿUmar \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} is narrated at
length in the books of Sīrah. Reflect upon it: a man as resolute as ʿUmar
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} set out with the intention of killing the Messenger
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, sword hanging at his neck. On the way, someone asked him
where he was going. He replied, “To kill the claimant of Prophethood.” The man
said, “First look to your own household.”

This was an indication that his sister had accepted Islam. He went to her house.
After some exchange, he began beating his brother-in-law. His sister rushed
forward to intervene, but in his anger, he did not spare her---she too was
struck, her face bloodied, her clothes stained.

Yet the final words uttered by that brave and resolute woman were, “O ʿUmar! Do
whatever you wish. This is not a state that can be undone. Once this neck has
entered into the collar of Allah's commands, it cannot be removed.” \[Madārij
al-Nubuwwah\]

Lubaynah was a slave-woman of ʿUmar \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. She had accepted Islam
before he himself embraced the faith. The condition of ʿUmar \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
at that time was such that he would begin beating her; he would continue
striking until he became exhausted, then sit down and say, “Let me rest a little
so that I may beat you again.” Yet observe the resolve of this strong-hearted
woman, she would reply, “O my master! If you do not accept Islam, then the True
Lord will deal with you in the same manner.” Alongside this zeal, behold her
reliance on Allah: she endured worldly suffering with the firm conviction that
the True Bestower would take retribution if he did not accept Islam.
\[Al-Iṣābah\]

Among the slave-women of Banū Makhzūm was one named Zinnīrah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}. She accepted faith in the early days of Islam. The Quraysh
subjected her to every possible form of torture, to the extent that she lost her
eyesight. Abū Jahl would say to her, “Zinnīrah! See! This has happened due to
the wrath of Lāt and ʿUzzā. You abandoned your ancestral religion, and they have
blinded you.” She replied, “What do these lifeless clods of earth and
hand-fashioned idols know of who persists in falsehood and who has submitted to
the truth? And if they possess no knowledge, how can they give punishment or
reward? Losing sight is a decree of Allah; if He wills, He can restore it.” It
is reported that her eyesight was later restored. \[Al-Kāmil of Ibn al-Athīr\]

Among the women who embraced Islam in its earliest days was a woman named
Ḥumnah. She was a servant of a Qurashī man. He would bind her tightly and
deprive her of food and drink. Despite these hardships, she did not turn away
from her true religion. Abū Bakr \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} paid her ransom and set her
free. \[Ṭabaqāt Ibn Saʿd\]

Umm Ḥabībah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, daughter of Abū Sufyān, migrated to
Abyssinia with her husband. Her husband later became a Christian, yet she did
not abandon Islam. \[Al-Iṣābah\]

O women of Islam! Reflect upon these examples placed before you. These were
events from a time when Islam had no supporters, when poverty and hunger
prevailed, when wealth and resources were scarce. Yet within them was a firm
heart that enabled them to endure all hardships and secured for them a lasting
place in history. It is now your duty to cultivate the same firmness, resolve,
determination, and steadfastness in the commands of Islam, so that history may
be filled with your legacy and the present decline and humiliation of Muslims
may come to an end.
