
## **The Blessings Brought About by Women**

Within the Sharīʿah of Islam, there are many rulings whose foundations were laid
in response to the requests and appeals of women. Numerous beneficial and
advantageous laws and principles owe their origin to the initiative of the
gentle gender.

In present-day India, there is frequent interaction with people of other faiths;
gifts are exchanged, and offerings are sent from one party to another. The
practice of accepting gifts from non-Muslims was established through the action
of a woman. Qutaylah bint ʿAbd al-ʿUzzā, who had previously been married to Abū
Bakr [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}. As she had not accepted Islam, he divorced her.
On one occasion, she brought some gifts, but due to her being a non-Muslim, Abū
Bakr declined them. Saddened, she then sent a message to ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله
عنها]{dir="rtl"}[^10], who consulted the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}. At that moment,
the verse was revealed:

[لَا يَنْهَاكُمُ اللّٰهُ عَنِ الَّذِينَ لَمْ يُقَاتِلُوكُم]{dir="rtl"}ْ

*Allah does not forbid you from those who do not fight you...* \[Sūrat
al-Mumtaḥanah, Q60:8\]

From this, the permissibility of accepting gifts from non-Muslims was
established.

Today, during travel and hardship, we benefit immensely from the blessing of
*tayammum*. But has it ever been considered what led to the revelation of this
immensely significant blessing? It was revealed due to an incident involving
ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}. During the expedition of Banū al-Muṣṭaliq,
her necklace was lost. The people went searching for it, and much time was spent
in this effort. The time for *ṣalāh* approached, yet no water was available at
all. People became greatly distressed. Some complained to Abū Bakr [رضي الله
عنه]{dir="rtl"} that this difficulty had arisen because of ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله
عنها]{dir="rtl"}. At that moment, the *āyah* of *tayammum* was revealed.

Usayd ibn Ḥuḍayr [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} remarked, "O family of Abū Bakr, this
is not your first blessing."

After the revelation of the *āyah*, when a camel was made to rise, the lost
necklace of ʿĀʾishah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} was found beneath it. \[Ṣaḥīḥ
al-Bukhārī\]

During the Prophetic era, the Treaty of Ḥudaybiyyah held immense importance. The
completion of this treaty was achieved through the counsel of a woman. The
Companions were somewhat distressed by the conditions of the treaty. The Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} instructed them to perform sacrifice at Ḥudaybiyyah, but they
delayed in carrying out the command. Saddened, the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}
returned to his tent.

Umm Salamah [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} advised him, "Perform the sacrifice
yourself first, and the others will follow." The Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} acted
upon this advice. He came out of the tent, had the animals brought, and
sacrificed them. Thereafter, all the Companions followed and performed their
sacrifices. \[Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī\]

Those who do not permit women any role in political affairs or governance should
reflect upon this: how the Beloved of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} acted upon the
counsel of a woman, and through that counsel brought such a significant matter
to completion in the most excellent manner.

The well-established rule of inheritance states that when both men and women are
among the heirs, the share of a male is twice that of a female. This principle
too was established by the appeal of a woman.

After the death of Aws ibn Thābit, his male relatives seized the estate. He had
two daughters who were deprived of the inheritance. His wife, Umm Kujjah [رضي
الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}, was still alive. She came to the court of the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} and lodged a complaint. At that moment, the ruling was revealed:

[لِلذَّكَرِ مِثْلُ حَظِّ الْأُنْثَيَيْنِ]{dir="rtl"}

*For the male is a share equal to that of two females.* \[Sūrat al-Nisāʾ,
Q4:11\][^11]

Similarly, a ruling regarding the separation of a mother and child was
established due to a woman's suffering. A slave woman named Umm Ḍamīrah was
separated from her child when sold. Upon seeing her weeping, the Prophet
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} decreed, "A mother and her child must not be separated (in a
sale)." \[Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī\]

At times, entire tribes embraced Islam through the *daʿwah* of women. Safānah
bint Ḥātim [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} was brought as a captive and appealed to
the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, mentioning her father's generosity. She said, "My
father used to ransom captives and free them. Now I feel ashamed to ransom
myself. Your mercy, O Messenger of Allah [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, is my intercessor." He
[ﷺ]{dir="rtl"} released her and prayed for her father. Upon returning to her
tribe, she proclaimed, "How unfortunate that you turn away from the one who is
the embodiment of mercy and compassion." Her tribe responded to her call and
embraced Islam. \[Al-Iṣābah\]

Another similar incident is recorded in *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī* in the *Book of
Tayammum;* ʿImrān ibn Ḥusayn [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} narrates that once, while
returning from a journey, they faced severe difficulty due to lack of water. No
sign of water could be found for miles. They went out searching and encountered
a woman carrying a water-skin. It was learned that water was located a full
day's journey away. She was brought to the Prophet [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, who
supplicated for blessings in the water. Everyone drank to satisfaction, and she
was then sent back with provisions.

Her tribe had not yet accepted Islam. Upon returning, she would say, "The
claimant of prophethood is either a magician or truly the Messenger of Allah."
The Muslims thereafter refrained from confronting her tribe, even while engaging
other tribes. Observing this conduct, she reflected deeply and eventually called
her people, saying, "Do you not see how mercifully these people treat us? Would
you not like to enter this blessed community?" The tribe accepted her
invitation, and all of them embraced Islam.

Individual *daʿwah* efforts by women are also numerous. Saʿdiyyah [رضي الله
عنها]{dir="rtl"}, an eloquent woman of Quraysh and ʿUthmān's [رضي الله
عنه]{dir="rtl"} aunt, advised him in such a compelling manner that he accepted
Islam.

ʿIkrimah ibn Abī Jahl [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}, who later displayed great
courage against the Romans and Persians during the caliphate of Umar ibn
al-Khattab [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}, had a wife (Umm Hakim bint al-Harith [رضي
الله عنها]{dir="rtl"}) who accepted Islam before him. ʿIkrimah fled toward Yemen
(after the Muslims marched in victoriously at Fatḥ Makkah). His wife sought
protection for him from the Messenger [ﷺ]{dir="rtl"}, then went to Yemen and
said to him, "You flee from one who brings the elixir of reform and guidance."
He returned to Makkah due to her words and accepted Islam. \[Sīrat Ibn Hishām\]

After the death of Mālik, the father of Anas ibn Mālik [رضي الله
عنه]{dir="rtl"}, his mother Umm Sulaym [رضي الله عنها]{dir="rtl"} wished to
remarry. Abū Ṭalḥah [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"} proposed to her, but she refused
on the basis that he was a polytheist. She said, "You worship idols that possess
no power to benefit nor harm, while I worship a Being whose power surpasses all
powers. What relation can there be between you and me?" This response was
delivered with such force and clarity that Abū Ṭalḥah [رضي الله عنه]{dir="rtl"}
was deeply affected and embraced Islam. \[Al-Iṣābah\]
